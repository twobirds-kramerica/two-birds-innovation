## Who Actually Hires a Fractional AI Consultant

### Primary: The Library or Nonprofit Director

**Profile:**
- Public library system director/manager OR nonprofit program director (settlement agency, community services, literacy org) in Ontario
- Annual digital budget: $20K–$100K; often grant-funded (NHSP, CanCode, ISED, Ontario Creates)
- Accountable to a board, a town council, or funders — every engagement needs a clear deliverable and justification
- Has been told by provincial/federal partners they "need to do something with AI" but no internal expertise to execute

**Decision path:**
1. Hears about a pilot or peer referral at an OLA or CULC conference
2. Googles "AI consulting for libraries Ontario" or "digital transformation nonprofit Canada"
3. Lands on site — immediately asks: "Is this person real? Have they done this for someone like me?"
4. Procurement gate: under $10K can often skip RFP (direct award in Ontario Broader Public Sector Procurement guidelines)
5. Needs a fixed-price, scoped deliverable (audit/report) to justify budget request to board
6. Timeline: 4–12 weeks from awareness to signed statement of work

**Biggest friction:** Generic "AI consultant" language. They need to see: libraries, nonprofits, public sector — not enterprise / startup / SaaS. A vague "we help organisations with AI" reads as "this person has never worked with a library board."

**Primary trust signals:**
- Named outcomes from similar organisations (even a single pilot/testimonial is enough)
- NHSP / CanCode / Canadian grant alignment in language
- Canadian data sovereignty assurance (they answer to privacy commissioners)
- Fixed price with clear deliverable — no hourly billing surprises
- Bio that shows public-sector or mission-driven work in the background

---

### Secondary: The Southwestern Ontario SME Owner

**Profile:**
- Manufacturing, professional services, trades, or retail — 5–100 employees
- Elgin County, Middlesex County, or broader SWON corridor
- Hears about AI from trade associations, BIA newsletters, or Chamber of Commerce
- Competitive pressure: larger competitors or US peers are automating workflows
- Does not understand AI — but understands "save 4 hours a week" and "reduce invoicing errors by 30%"

**Decision path:**
1. Referral from BIA / Chamber / peer, or finds site on Google (local search)
2. Scans homepage in 30–45 seconds — needs to see: "this is for businesses like mine," a specific dollar figure, and a low-friction next step
3. Decides in one conversation (owner culture — no committee, no RFP)
4. Price sensitivity is real: $2,500 is in the "worth trying" zone; $10,000+ requires a ROI case

**Biggest friction:** Feels like AI consultants are for "tech companies" or "big corporations" — not for a 15-person cabinet shop in St. Thomas. Needs geographic anchor and ordinary-language descriptions.

**Primary trust signals:**
- "Southwestern Ontario" or city-level specificity
- Before/after outcome language (hours saved, errors eliminated, revenue recovered)
- Fixed price + 30-day delivery — no open-ended commitment
- Real person with a face and local connection
- Entry point that feels safe: "Book a 30-min call" not "Submit a proposal"

### Tertiary: The Grant Reviewer / Institutional Partner

**Profile:**
- NHSP program officer, Ontario Creates assessor, municipality's digital innovation lead, or HR at a post-secondary institution
- Evaluating Two Birds as an eligible service provider or technology partner
- Needs: organisational legitimacy, track record, privacy/data policy, Canadian incorporation

**Decision path:**
- Finds the site via a referral in a grant application or through ISED/federal partner network
- Reads the company profile or about page — scans for: legal name, address, founder background, and product/service descriptions
- Does not need a sales pitch — needs documentation

**Trust signals:**
- Grants company profile page (already built: `grants/company-overview.html`)
- Physical address (business mailing address — St. Thomas, ON)
- Canadian registration implied by GST/HST registration and Ontario incorporation (if filed)
- Privacy policy with PIPEDA compliance statement
- No inflated claims ("20+ years of experience" not "industry-leading AI pioneer")

## What They Actually Decide On

**Library/nonprofit institutional path (B2G):**
- Entry engagement: fixed-price AI Workflow Audit ($2,500 CAD) — scoped, deliverable in 30 days, justifiable to board
- Follow-on: Fractional AI Leadership ($4,000/month, 1 day/week, month-to-month) or DCC white-label ($2,500 pilot + $10K–$25K/year)
- Conversion driver: specificity of deliverable + peer organisation reference
- Conversion killer: generic AI language, no NHSP/grant alignment, opaque pricing

**SME path:**
- Entry engagement: AI Workflow Audit ($2,500) — "show me where I'm losing time/money" framing
- Follow-on: implementation sprint or fractional retainer
- Conversion driver: local anchor, outcome specificity, low-risk fixed price
- Conversion killer: Toronto pricing signal, enterprise language, no face/name on the site

**Rate context (Signal & Form, chatgpt.ca, Aries Consulting 2026):**
- AI readiness audit market range: $2,000–$8,000 CAD for SMB; $5,000–$15,000 mid-market
- Two Birds Audit at $2,500 is the low end of credible range — positions as accessible, not cheap
- Fractional at $4,000/month is below most Toronto-based fractional AI offers ($6,000–$10,000/month)
- Competitive advantage: Southwestern Ontario cost base; not billing Bay Street rent

| Dimension | Generic Toronto AI Consultants | Large Advisory (Deloitte/KPMG) | AI Forward (aiforward.ca) | Two Birds |

| Geography | Toronto / national | National / US | Toronto / remote | Southwestern Ontario + remote |
| Verticals | SaaS, fintech, ecommerce | Financial services, government | Various SMB | Libraries, nonprofits, manufacturing, trades |
| Founder visible? | Usually not | No | Yes (Shekhar Dewan bio) | Yes (Aaron Patzalek — must be more visible) |
| Case studies | Usually yes | Yes | Sparse | None yet (key gap) |
| Grant alignment | Rare | No | No | Yes (NHSP language, DCC case study) |

**Positioning conclusion:**
Two Birds cannot compete on "largest AI consulting firm in Canada." The differentiator is *accessible expertise for mission-driven and SWON organisations* — the same rigor as a Toronto firm, without the Bay Street pricing, with a consultant who has actually built the tools (DCC, KevsCasa, Career Coach) and understands the library/community sector.

The build-proof advantage (HAL Stack, DCC, KevsCasa deployed and live) is unusually credible for a solo consultant — most fractional AI consultants sell strategy, not demonstrated product delivery. The site needs to show the work, not just describe the offer.

**Aaron's biggest gap relative to competitors: zero case studies, zero named client outcomes.** A single named pilot (even a beta/discounted engagement) would move the site from "plausible" to "credible."

## Tone + Voice Guidelines

**Do:**
- Geographic anchor: "Southwestern Ontario" named explicitly, not just "Canada"
- Outcome-first: "Your team saves 4 hours a week" not "We leverage cutting-edge AI solutions"
- Pricing visible: "$2,500 CAD flat" on the page — removes the biggest institutional friction point
- Build proof: link to live products (DCC, KevsCasa, Clarity) as evidence of delivery capacity
- Fixed-price framing: "Scoped. Delivered. Done." — not open-ended consulting language

**Avoid:**
- "Cutting-edge," "leverage," "synergy," "transformative," "next-level"
- Americanisms: "defense" → "defence", "honor" → "honour", "program" → "programme" (in educational contexts)
- "AI pioneer" or any unverifiable superlative
- Toronto-scale enterprise language ("enterprise transformation," "portfolio of clients")
- Passive claims: "we help organisations" → "Aaron builds" or "your team gets"
